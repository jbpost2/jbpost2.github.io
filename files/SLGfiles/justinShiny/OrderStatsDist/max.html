Distribution of the Maximum Order Statistic

We can use the cdf method to find the distribution of \(Y_{(n)}\). $$F_{Y_{(n)}}(y)=P(Y_{(n)}\leq y)$$ For the largest to be less than or equal to \(y\), we need all \(Y_i\) to be less than or equal to \(y\). $$\implies = P(Y_1\leq y,Y_2\leq y,...,Y_n\leq y)$$ $$ = P(Y_1\leq y)P(Y_2\leq y)...P(Y_n\leq Y)$$ (since the RVs are independent) $$= F_{Y_1}(y)F_{Y_2}(y)...F_{Y_n}(y) = \left[F_Y(y)\right]^n$$ (since the RVs are identically distributed). Now if we want the pdf we can differentiate (we must use the chain rule here): $$f_{Y_{(n)}}(y)=\frac{d}{dy}\left[F_{Y}(y)\right]^n = n\left[F_Y(y)\right]^{n-1}f_Y(y)$$

We can also think about this pdf from a "counting" persepective.
  • n = # of ways to select which sample member is the largest.
  • \(\left[F_Y(y)\right]^{n-1}\) corresponds to having n-1 of the sample members less than the max.
  • \(f_y(y)\) is the pdf for the sample member that is the max.

Example: \(Y_i^{iid}\sim Beta(2,2)\)

$$f_Y(y)=6y(1-y)~~~0 < y < 1~~~~~~~~~~~~~F_Y(y)=3y^2-2y^3~~~~0 < y < 1$$ Distribution of the max is then $$f_{Y_{(n)}}(y)=n\left[3y^2-2y^3\right]^{n-1}6y(1-y) = 6n(3-2y)^{n-1}y^{2n-1}(1-y)~~~~~0 < y < 1$$ Note:
  • if \(n=1\) we get that the max follows a Beta(2,2), why does this make sense?
